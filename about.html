Introduction to Normandy?


Normandy (Normandie) is a historic and cultural region of northern France which comprises of the provinces Manche, Calvados, Orne, Eure, and Seine-Maritime.
Previously Normandy was divided into two regions “Basse-Normandie” and “Haute-Normandie”,
however in 2016 these regions were re-united to become one.
Parts of Normandy consist of rolling countryside typified by pasture for dairy cattle and apple orchards.


What is Normandy famous for?


Unfortunately, Normandy is very famous for World War II (it’s another topic).
The region of Normandy is famous for its cider which is an alcoholic beverage made from the fermented juice of apples. Cider has been made in Normandy since the 11th century and it is one of the main exports of this area. Like champagnes and crémants, cider also comes in various levels of sweetness.
There is so many different varieties of Apple in Normandy,
they are green, red, sweet, crunchy, sour used in making beautiful Tarte Tatin , Cider and the reputed Calvados.
There is also a wide variety of Pears and Normans make Perry beverage alongside beautiful desserts.

The history of gastronomy in Normandy?


Normandy is the native land of Taillevent, cook of the kings of France Charles V and Charles VI. He wrote the earliest French cookery book named Le Viandier.
Normandy has some of the best ingredients and always been a dream foodie destination.

Famous for producing Haute-Cuisine, it’s been a leader in providing some of France’s most indulgent and recognisable dishes for centuries.
It is not surprising that Normandy cuisine has a distinct identity with its own favourite ingredients: apples, cider, cheeses, cream, butter and more.
A wide range of dairy products are produced and exported.
Normandy butter and Normandy cream are lavishly used in gastronomic specialties.

Jersey and Guernsey cattle are famous cattle breeds worldwide, especially to North America.
Turbot and oysters from the Cotentin Peninsula are major delicacies throughout France.
Normandy is the chief oyster-cultivating, scallop-exporting, and mussel-raising region in France.

Fun fact

Normandy is the largest producer of “huîtres creuses” in France.''
so much so that one in every three oysters consumed in France comes from Normandy.

Normandy is a major cider-producing region (very little wine is produced). brandy, of which the most famous variety is calvados, is also popular.
The mealtime trou normand, or "Norman hole", is a pause between meal courses in which diners partake of a glassful of calvados in order to improve the appetite and make room for the next course, and this is still observed in many homes and restaurants.
Pommeau is an apéritif produced by blending unfermented cider and apple brandy.
Another aperitif is the kir normand, a measure of crème de cassis topped up with cider. Bénédictine is produced in Fécamp.

Normandy dishes include:

- Marmitte dieppoise.
- Duckling à la rouennaise.
- sautéed chicken yvetoise.
- Rabbit cooked with morels.
- Goose filet in a mushrooms cream sauces.

Normandy is also known for its pastries:

- Douillons (pears baked in pastry),
- craquelins, roulettes in Rouen,
- fouaces in Caen,
- fallues in Lisieux,
- sablés in Lisieux.

Normandy is the birthplace of the brioche (especially those from Evreux and Gisors).

Normandy is also known for is Confectionery:

- Apple sugar in Rouen.
- Caramels in Isigny.
- Mint chews Bayeux.
- Berlingots in Falaise.
- Marzipans in Le Havre.
- Croquettes Argentan.
- Macaroons in Rouen.

Confiture de lait was also made in Normandy around the 14th century.
